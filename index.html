Robots in the IoT

The Terraswarm Research Center, UC Berkeley

Robots connected to the Internet of Things (IoT) would be able to utilize a variety of sensors and actuators in their environment to perform a task. However, a robotic system and an IoT environment are not easy to merge.

A robotic system operates best in a controlled environment with reliable sensors. An IoT environment however, can be dynamic and connecting to new devices across different communication protocols.

In this project, I developed accessors for the Ptolemy softwarethat communicated with ROS. Ptolemy is a software model developed at UC Berkeley for designing embedded systems.ROS (Robot Operating System) is a software architecture suited for robotic communications and algorithms. In Ptolemy, accessors wrap sensors, actuators, and services into a formalized model of computation. The accessors were the interface between the robot and its surrounding devices.

In a demo for the project, I programmed a robot to patrol several objects in an area, and control a set of lights. I connected a few Philips smart Hue bulbs and an IP camera to a Local Area Network. The IP camera located AprilTags, which marked certain objects-of-interest in the robots environment. Ptolemy would connect to these devices, and send the coordinates of the tags to the robot as waypoints. It would then monitor the journey of the robot toward the objects, and turning on a light once the robot arrived. This successfuly demonstrated the integration of ROS and Ptolemy for an IoT robotic application.

Directed Audio Traffic Warning Speaker

GRASP Lab, Penn

Traffic intersections are dangerous places for pedestrians, especially for the visually impaired. Some pedestrian traffic lights, emit a warning sound to alert blind pedestrians when it's time to cross.

However, regular audio spreads out in all directions, and is not easily localized. My teammates and I at the GRASP lab, are designing a directional speaker that emits sound in a narrow beam. The sound can only be heard by specific pedestrians, as it attenuates sharply outside its coverage zone. Thus a blind pedestrian would be able to track the location of the speaker more closely.

Directed audio is modulated at high frequencies (40 kHz) to narrow its beam of coverage. Although the carrier frequency is inaudible, as the signal travels through air, difference frequencies are generated that reproduce the audible sound.

We are can combine directed audio with pedestrian detection sensors to create a smart intersection system. It is safer for pedestrians, prevents collisions for drivers, and reduces noise for the general public.
